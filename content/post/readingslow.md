As a studious kid in school, I have always looked out for ways to improve my learning efficiency. During the 
scramble for exam preparations, I came across a "speed reading" course. I got the course material delivered home in no time. 
It was a bunch of weird exercises like reading  
inverted pages and reading without enunciating the words. Soon after, I was almost unable to read anything
at all. 

Often, we come across speed readers. The biography of Franklin D. Roosevelt claims that he read a book
every single day. I have a friend who once read a 10,000 page fan fiction of Harry Potter in 8 days. 
How awesome it would be to scan through 300 pages in a couple of hours. With my speed I won't finish 
all the books I want to read
in my lifetime. No technique has ever helped me. Now, I have come to terms with my slow reading: very good terms.

"The tortoise inched steadily and outstripped the callous hare while it took a nap." Some would skim through those words 
only to get to the next event in the story. Some would momentarily spawn a vivid visualization of it. They 
would have their own cinematic perspective---the tortoise looking back at the hare in slow motion. 
To slow readers like
me, it is neither of these. A hundred sleeping hares from my life's experiences emerge in front of me all at the same time. 
I have to choose between the literal and the figurative and stack the information in my mind's shelves for
information and for appreciation of language. I reread to reflect. I hunt my memory to check if this information 
is a branch of a thought tree that was thriving 
for a long time.  The pause is sometimes more than just a moment.

The attempt to read faster took me farther from myself. I have realized that my way of reading
doesn't drift with the author. Instead, it is simply a process in the composition of my life's experience. Hence
a slow reader.
