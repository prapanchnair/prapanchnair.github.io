India's higher education seems to be going through a substantial overhaul. Through 
the 5 years I pursued my PhD from 2010 to 2015, the stipend increased three folds.  
Major institutions are opening up to attract foreign faculty and there are now 
scholarship programs payings on par with a job in a multinational company. 
However, in every field of education, there exists a huge chasm between the tier 
1 institutes and the rest. The top ranked institutions in every field which are often in constant
academic exchange with foreign institutes make original 
contributions. As we 
move down the rankings, the decline in quality is alarming. There are academic networks where 
a thesis that merely 
passes a plagiarism test will earn you a PhD. 
The extra resources our government pumps into the tier 1 institutes may or may 
not *trickle down* to the rest of the country just as we are unsure of economic trickle down.
Clearly, the brilliant minds of the country are far larger than the limited 
positions in tier 1 institutions picked through their *one-dimensional* screening process. 
What then is preventing the best minds of our country affiliated with the ordinary 
institutions of India from motivating themselves and improve their own standards
when all the material resources necessary are in place? Is there a common factor 
in our inherent teaching method that make us not win a Nobel Prize? I believe there 
is at least one. Loyalty.

Remember the first time you learned Newton's first law, when you were taught 
word by word that an object travelling with a *constant* velocity in a *straight* line
continues to *remain* unless you intervene? You lost a point for missing each 
of those key words. We and our teachers equally believed in them because those were
elements of a law of nature. Later we learned that these laws are applicable within 
a *classical* framework and outside of this the law loses its generality and that 
space and time can curve we don't even know if anything ever truly travels in a straight line. Ever.
Yet we call it a law. Similarly we have the so called scientific 'discoveries'
until they stop remaining discoveries or get modified as we explore more about them.
While the Indian mind beats itself in succumbing to this loyalty to the laws and 
discoveries, the rest of the world does not seem to take these words literally. Somehow, 
a school student in Germany understands that any written equation is simply a model
that holds true only within the limits of our understanding at that point in time. 
That young researcher in the US tests the theory of evolution in his backyard as if 
he did not understand the literal meaning of the word discovery.  But the 
college goer in India is
 loyal. If something 
is a law or a discovery, how can we probe it? Law is supposed to be obeyed and 
a discovery is a truth. There is immense trust on the source of these. 
We believe our
success and acceptance in the world depends on this loyalty. But we were only 
trying to understand, you may argue. Tell me, don't we truly understand a gadget 
when we break it first?
